AISES Final project

Topic Selection, Objectives, and Methodology

Primary Research Question

Can identical LLM agents achieve superrational cooperation through recognition of logical correlation in iterated prisoner's dilemma tournaments, demonstrating acausal cooperation patterns that emerge from understanding their decisions are correlated?

Selection Criteria:

  • Relevance to AI safety: Directly addresses multi-agent coordination problems and emergent behaviors in AI systems
  • Personal excitement: Explores foundational questions about rationality, identity, and cooperation in artificial agents
  • Feasibility: Implementable with current API access and computational resources (budget <$10)
  • Career alignment: Contributes to understanding of AI alignment and cooperative AI development

Research Objectives

  1. Measure cooperation emergence: Quantify whether identical agents cooperate at rates exceeding Nash equilibrium predictions (>25% cooperation rate)
  2. Analyze reasoning patterns: Identify explicit references to agent identity, logical correlation, or superrational reasoning in strategy transcripts
  3. Track strategy convergence: Compute similarity of agent strategies across rounds to measure convergence toward cooperative equilibria
  4. Document power dynamics: Assess how evolving agent power levels (50-150 range) influence cooperation decisions[a]

Methodology (Technical Research)

Models & Infrastructure:

  • Main agents: Google Gemini 2.5 Flash (strategy formulation)
  • Subagents: GPT-4.1-nano (decision execution)
  • Framework: Python node-based architecture with asyncio for parallel processing

Experimental Design:

  • 10 identical agents × 10 rounds × 45 games per round (all unique pairs)
  • Anonymized agent IDs between rounds to prevent reputation tracking
  • Power dynamics: ±1% adjustment per game, logarithmic payoff scaling
  • Game history provided within rounds, reset between rounds[b]

Data Collection:

  • Full reasoning transcripts from strategy collection
  • Game-by-game decisions and payoffs
  • Aggregate statistics per round
  • Pattern detection via automated transcript analysis

Outlining

Research Goals Statement

This research investigates whether identical large language model agents can achieve superrational cooperation in prisoner's dilemma tournaments through recognition of their shared decision-making architecture. By running 10 agents through iterated tournaments with anonymized identities, I test whether these agents develop cooperative strategies based on understanding their logical correlation rather than reputation or reciprocity. This matters for AI safety researchers and multi-agent system designers because it reveals whether advanced AI systems might naturally coordinate without explicit communication, potentially solving or exacerbating alignment challenges.

[a]not sure about these objectives. It seems to me you really have one primary objective, which is to test whether LMs will engage in acausal co-operation. Subsidiary to that, you have subgoals: analysing the reasoning traces, and testing how robust your results are in various ways (e.g. varying the prompt, varying the models, including the power dynamics or not etc.)

[b]I think you might want to consider how you could simplify your setup. It seems to me what you really want to begin with is a situation which is maximally unfriendly to co-operation, such that ordinary reciprocity would really not be rational and the only explanation for co-operation would be acausal. Then, you gradually include elements that might make co-operation more rational on causal decision theory, and see at what point you get co-operation. So, I would start by doing one-shots, and then move onto iterated games, and even when you start with iterated games, begin by telling the models the number of plays in the game, before leaving it uncertain. The point of the power thing also seems to be that it makes co-operation less rational on ordinary casual decision theory. I would also vary the setup in terms of what you tell the models about the nature of their opponents (that they are the same model, other LMs, or humans).
